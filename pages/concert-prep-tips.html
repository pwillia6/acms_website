Preparing For A Performance

By Lorraine Moxey (Original publication, January 1992)

Performing is the most rewarding thing a musician can do. However, a successful chamber recital demands thorough pacing and group discipline. Review these steps to prepare your mind and body for the stage.

Buffer your preparation: Allow a full three months of preparation for an unfamiliar chamber piece assuming 1 rehearsal per week.

Pick manageable limits: A simple, beautiful movement performed flawlessly is infinitely better than a complex, frantic movement played poorly.

Erase distractions: Wipe clean any prior pencil markings or fingerings before rehearsals begin, highlighting tricky transitions with a soft pencil icon.

Slow, Soft Tuning: Always tune softly, particularly string players. Once in tune, sit quietly. Tuning is not a time to perform personal cadenzas.

Establish Seating Lines: Place chairs so that peripheral vision connects all musicians. Low music stands prevent barrier blocks.

Tolerate Constructive Critique: Chamber groups are ultimate democracies. Try disputes both ways and make determinations objectively. Do not let egos damage music.

Taming Physicality: Shaking limbs, perspiration, and dry throat are perfectly natural adrenaline responses. Nerves give performance an incredible 'edge' if focused positively.

Breathe Before Starting: When sitting on stage, take 5 extra seconds. Breathe slowly to steady your pulse. Establish key templates mentally before the leader bows.

Mistake Immunity: Never grimace or frown when a part is dropped. 90% of the audience never notices; the remaining 10% are rooting for you. Put double the energy into the following bar!
